Korean Wedding Customs

Korean weddings blend long-standing Confucian family rituals with the streamlined logistics of modern wedding halls. Many couples now hold a brief Western-style ceremony first, then a separate traditional segment that honors both families.

1. Hanbok and Wedding Attire

For traditional segments, the bride wears a colorful hanbok — commonly a green jeogori (jacket) over a red chima (skirt) — while the groom wears a formal hanbok in blue or earth tones. Western gowns and tuxedos are used for the main hall ceremony, then guests see the couple change into hanbok before the paebaek.

2. Modern Wedding Halls and Timing

Most urban weddings happen in dedicated wedding halls that schedule multiple ceremonies per day. The main ceremony is short (often 30 minutes), followed by group photos and a buffet meal. Tight timing is normal: families coordinate arrivals carefully so each step fits the venue’s slot.

3. Paebaek: The Traditional Family Ceremony

Paebaek is a private Confucian ritual where the couple bows to the parents and elders, presents jujubes and chestnuts, and receives blessings. Parents toss the fruit into the bride’s skirt, symbolizing wishes for children and prosperity. It is one of the most emotional and family-focused parts of the day.

4. Symbolic Gifts and Etiquette

Gift culture is structured. Guests typically give cash in white envelopes (chukuigeum) at a reception desk before the ceremony. Between families, items such as ham (a gift box delivered to the bride’s family before the wedding) carry historical meaning even when contents have been modernized.

5. Contemporary Trends

Recent trends include “small weddings” (jageun gyeolhon) hosted in restaurants or chapels, photo-driven storytelling, and shorter timelines. Couples also lean on professional helpers (doumi) to coordinate transitions, so the day stays calm despite the dense schedule.
